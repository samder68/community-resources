ACA Coverage Checklist (2025)

Action Required: Your Coverage May Be At Risk

Recent changes mean enrollment periods are shorter and verification requirements are stricter. Relying on auto-renewal may result in losing your subsidies or being enrolled in a plan that no longer meets your needs. It is critical to actively review your options and submit all required documents on time to avoid a gap in your health coverage.

Key Dates & Deadlines

  • Open Enrollment Starts: November 1, 2025 [+ Add to Calendar]
  • Open Enrollment Ends: December 15, 2025 [+ Add to Calendar]
  • Special Enrollment: Apply within 30 days of a life change (like job loss).
  • Verification Deadline: Submit documents within 30 days of enrollment.

Documents You'll Need

Gather these documents to make enrollment smoother:

  • Proof of Income: Recent pay stubs, 2024 tax return, or unemployment letter.
  • Proof of Identity: Driver's license or other government-issued ID.
  • Proof of Residency/Citizenship: U.S. passport, green card, or work permit.
  • Health Insurance Info: Current plan details and any notices from Healthcare.gov.

Pro Tips

  • Don't rely on auto-renewal. Review your plan each year to ensure it still fits your needs.
  • Set calendar reminders for key enrollment dates.
  • Save all official emails and mail from the Health Insurance Marketplace.

Health Coverage Guide for Adults Age 55-64 (2025)

Action Required: Secure Your Health Coverage Before Medicare

The years leading up to Medicare eligibility can be a high-risk period for unexpected health issues and rising insurance costs. It is critical to secure comprehensive coverage during this time. Be cautious of plans that seem too good to be true, such as "health-sharing ministries," which are not real insurance and can leave you with massive bills. Making an informed choice now is vital for your health and financial security.

What To Do

  • Review ACA Plans Annually: Your needs and plan options can change, especially if your income is near a subsidy threshold.
  • Consider Health Savings Accounts (HSAs): If you choose a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), an HSA allows you to save tax-free money for medical costs.
  • Learn About 'Medicare Bridge' Options: These are private plans that can cover you in the years just before you become eligible for Medicare.
  • Beware of "Health-Sharing Ministries": These are not insurance, are unregulated, and can deny coverage for pre-existing conditions.

Pro Tips

  • Estimate your yearly healthcare needs before picking a plan—don't just focus on the monthly premium.
  • Keep a personal medical history record to make switching plans easier.
  • Ask your doctors which insurance plans they accept; you may find more affordable options.

Key Resources

Get reliable information and assistance.

Healthcare.gov: www.healthcare.gov

SHIP Counselors (Free, unbiased help): www.shiphelp.org

Medicare Basics: www.medicare.gov

Federal & SS Retirement Guide (2025)

Action Required: Your Retirement Benefits May Change

New federal budget proposals aim to limit cost-of-living adjustments (COLAs), raise the full retirement age, and potentially shift some federal retirement systems toward privatized models. These changes could significantly impact both current and future Social Security recipients and federal workers.

Steps to Take Now

  • Check your estimated Social Security benefits regularly via your online account.
  • Re-evaluate your retirement timeline, especially if you are under age 60.
  • Federal employees: Review your FERS/CSRS balance and pension projections carefully.
  • Talk to a trusted financial advisor about diversifying your retirement income streams (e.g., annuities, Roth IRAs).
  • Track congressional discussions that affect COLAs or federal retirement formulas.

Tips & Warnings

  • Delayed retirement credits may be reduced, meaning waiting to retire won't provide the same benefit increase as in the past.
  • Expect smaller COLAs and potentially tighter eligibility rules for disability claims.
  • Federal workers nearing retirement should closely monitor their Thrift Savings Plan (TSP) options and any proposed changes.

Resources & Hotlines

Use these official resources for planning and information.

  • Federal Retirement (TSP): tsp.gov
  • Federal Employee Advocacy: NARFE (National Active and Retired Federal Employees)
  • Social Security Hotline: 1-800-772-1213

Immigration & Asylum Access Guide (2025)

Action Required: Know Your Rights and Prepare for Delays

Recent budget changes are expected to slash asylum processing funds, expand detention, and increase deportation enforcement. These actions will likely reduce access to legal aid and significantly delay protection for vulnerable migrants. Proactive planning is essential.

Steps to Take Now

  • Seek legal counsel immediately if you or a loved one has an open immigration case.
  • Connect with local immigrant rights organizations now, before a crisis arises.
  • Gather and make copies of all important documents (passports, IDs, marriage certificates, proof of presence).
  • Avoid "notario" fraud. Only trust accredited legal representatives or attorneys.
  • Subscribe to updates from legal organizations monitoring changes in your area.

Tips & Warnings

  • Funding cuts will likely delay court dates and reduce access to free legal services. Plan for long waits.
  • Know your rights if you have an encounter with ICE. You have the right to remain silent and to speak with an attorney. Do not sign anything without legal help.
  • Document every interaction with immigration officials. Keep all notices, receipts, and records in a safe place.

Resources & Hotlines

Use these resources for free or low-cost legal help and information.

  • Free Legal Help: ImmigrationHelp.org
  • Know Your Rights: ACLU Immigrants' Rights Project
  • Legal Updates & Analysis: National Immigration Law Center (NILC)
  • Immigration Legal Hotline: 1-800-528-4044
  • Youth & DACA Hotline: United We Dream Hotline (1-844-363-1423)

Health Coverage After Job Loss (2025)

Action Required: You Have a Limited Time to Get New Coverage

Losing your job-based health insurance triggers a Special Enrollment Period, but this window is very short (often only 30-60 days). A gap in coverage, no matter how brief, exposes you to the full cost of any medical care you might need. Acting immediately to explore ACA Marketplace or Medicaid options is the only way to protect yourself and your family from potentially catastrophic medical bills.

Immediate Steps to Take

  • Act Fast: Losing job-based coverage qualifies you for a Special Enrollment Period on the ACA Marketplace, but you must apply quickly.
  • Explore ACA Plans: Visit Healthcare.gov to see what plans and subsidies you qualify for based on your new income.
  • Check Medicaid Eligibility: With a lower income, you may now qualify for Medicaid.
  • Compare with COBRA: COBRA lets you keep your old plan but is often much more expensive than an ACA plan with subsidies.

Pro Tips

  • Don't go without coverage, even for a short time. An emergency can be financially devastating.
  • ACA subsidies are based on your estimated *yearly* income, so be sure to include unemployment benefits when you apply.
  • Use free, official navigators to help you choose a plan. Avoid scam brokers.

Warning Signs of Scam Plans

  • Plans that don't mention the ACA or have a clear provider network.
  • Lack of coverage for prescriptions or emergencies.
  • Brokers who pressure you to enroll immediately.

Medicaid Redetermination Checklist (2025)

Action Required: You Must Renew Your Medicaid Coverage

States are now required to check every recipient's eligibility. Your Medicaid coverage is no longer guaranteed to renew automatically. If you do not respond to mail or online notices from your state's Medicaid office, your benefits could be terminated. It is essential to update your contact information and submit your renewal paperwork on time to avoid losing your health coverage.

Steps to Take for Renewal

  • Update Your Contact Info: Make sure your state Medicaid office has your current mailing address and phone number.
  • Watch Your Mail: Open all mail from your state's health department. Your renewal packet will be sent this way.
  • Respond Quickly: Return your renewal paperwork by the deadline, usually within 30 days.
  • Appeal if Denied: If you believe you are still eligible but get denied, you have the right to appeal.

Documents You'll Need

  • Proof of Identity: Driver's license, state ID, or birth certificate.
  • Proof of Income: Recent pay stubs, Social Security benefit letter, or unemployment statement.
  • Proof of Residency: A recent utility bill or lease agreement with your name and address.
  • Household Info: Names and birthdates of everyone in your household.

Pro Tips

  • Keep a copy or photo of every document you submit.
  • Use your state's online portal if available—it's often faster.
  • Respond even if you think your coverage auto-renews. Many rules have changed.

Need Help?

Contact your state's Medicaid office or the national helpline.

Phone: 1-877-267-2323

Website: www.medicaid.gov/renewals

Medicare Cuts Survival Guide (2025-2026)

Action Required: Your Medicare Benefits May Be Reduced

Planned annual cuts to Medicare are scheduled to begin in 2026. These changes could lead to higher out-of-pocket costs, reduced access to certain doctors, and changes in covered services. It is essential to review your current coverage, understand how these cuts might affect you, and prepare for potential changes during the upcoming Open Enrollment period.

Steps to Take Now

  • Review your current plan (Advantage or Part D) for any at-risk benefits.
  • Evaluate a Medigap plan to see if it could help cover potential new costs.
  • Reassess your prescription drug coverage, as some medications may no longer be covered.
  • Watch for official letters from Medicare or your plan administrator explaining changes.
  • Check your protections with your state Medicare office, especially if you are transgender, disabled, or receiving gender-affirming care.

Tips & Warnings

  • Cuts to provider payments may reduce access to doctors, especially in rural areas. Ask your doctor if they plan to remain in-network.
  • You may see higher co-pays or fewer approved visits for services like physical therapy, mental health, or home healthcare.
  • Do not auto-renew your plan. Review all changes during Open Enrollment (Oct 15 - Dec 7). [+ Add to Calendar]

Planned Parenthood & Reproductive Health Guide (2025)

Action Required: Your Access to Reproductive Health Services May Be At Risk

New legislation may affect providers like Planned Parenthood, impacting access to essential, non-abortion services including cancer screenings, STI testing, and birth control for Medicaid recipients. It is crucial to confirm service availability at your local clinic and prepare alternative plans for your reproductive healthcare needs.

Steps to Take Now

  • Call your clinic to confirm which services will be affected and if they still accept your insurance.
  • Locate alternative clinics or community health centers nearby using the resources below.
  • Schedule essential exams and contraception refills as soon as possible to avoid a lapse in care.
  • Consider long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) like IUDs or implants for longer-term coverage.
  • Explore telehealth and mail-order birth control options as a backup.

Tips & Warnings

  • Even if a clinic is targeted for providing abortions, many of its other health services are unrelated and critical for community health.
  • Telehealth providers and advocacy groups may offer temporary or low-cost solutions.
  • Local reproductive justice organizations often help coordinate care alternatives and may offer financial assistance.

Prescription Discount Guide (2025)

Action Required: Don't Overpay for Your Medication

The cost of prescription drugs can be a significant burden, even for those with insurance. Never assume the price you are first quoted is the lowest price available. Using discount programs and comparing pharmacies is not just for the uninsured; it is an essential step for everyone to ensure access to necessary medication without facing financial hardship. You must be your own advocate to find the best price.

How to Save on Medications

  • Use Discount Apps & Websites: Services like GoodRx, SingleCare, and CostPlus Drugs can offer prices lower than insurance copays.
  • Compare Pharmacy Prices: Use a discount app to see which local pharmacy has the best price for your prescription.
  • Ask for Generics: Generic versions are often significantly cheaper than brand-name drugs.
  • Check Manufacturer Programs: Drug manufacturers sometimes offer patient assistance programs (PAPs) for those with high costs.

Top Resources

Discount Programs:

  • GoodRx
  • CostPlus Drugs
  • SingleCare
  • Blink Health

Low-Cost Pharmacies:

  • Walmart ($4 generics list)
  • Costco (membership may not be needed for pharmacy)
  • Check local grocery store pharmacies for specials.

Pro Tips

  • You can't combine insurance with a discount coupon, but you can always ask the pharmacist to use whichever is cheaper.
  • Always ask the pharmacist for the price *before* paying.
  • If a medication is too expensive, ask your doctor if there is a less expensive therapeutic alternative.

Need More Help?

For help finding patient assistance programs:

Partnership for Prescription Assistance: 1-888-477-2669

Website: www.pparx.org

Rural Hospital Closures Survival Guide (2025)

Action Required: Your Local Hospital May Be At Risk

Rural hospitals are closing at record rates due to funding cuts and workforce shortages. This poses a direct threat to emergency, maternity, and general care for entire communities. It is critical to identify alternative care options and prepare a plan before you face a medical emergency.

Steps to Take Now

  • Identify the nearest alternative hospitals or urgent care centers and verify they are open.
  • Ask your provider if they will continue practicing locally or are relocating.
  • Discuss transportation plans with family in case of a medical emergency.
  • Make a proactive care plan if you are pregnant or managing a chronic illness.
  • Contact your state representatives to advocate for rural healthcare funding.

Tips & Warnings

  • Hospital closures often lead to longer ER wait times and ambulance delays.
  • Do not rely on online maps for facility status in rural areas—always call to verify.
  • Look into telehealth services that accept your insurance or Medicare.

SNAP (Food Assistance) Guide (2025)

Action Required: Know the New SNAP Rules

Stricter work requirements for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) are now in effect. Many adults will need to provide proof of work, training, or volunteer hours to maintain their food assistance. Incomplete applications or missed appointments can lead to a loss of benefits. It is vital to understand the new rules, log your hours, and respond to all communications from your state SNAP office.

Steps to Take

  • Check new work requirements: Adults aged 18-64 may need to prove 80 hours per month of work, training, or volunteer activity.
  • Gather your documents: Collect pay stubs, training program verification, or proof of exemption (e.g., disability, caring for a child).
  • Apply or renew as early as possible. Processing delays are possible.
  • Track all communications from your state's SNAP office and respond immediately.
  • If you are denied, seek help. Call 211 or your local food bank for assistance with appeals or emergency food.

Pro Tips

  • Start logging your work, volunteer, or training hours now.
  • Check your state's specific list of exemptions from the work requirement. You may qualify without realizing it.
  • You can reapply even if you have been denied before. Don't give up.

Help, Legal Aid & Advocacy

These resources can help you apply, appeal a decision, or find emergency food assistance.

  • Find Your State SNAP Office: SNAP State Directory
  • General Help & Food Assistance: Call 211 or visit 211.org
  • National Hunger Hotline: 1-866-3-HUNGRY

Social Security Preparation Guide (2025)

Action Required: Prepare for Your Financial Future

Potential policy shifts and economic uncertainty make it more important than ever to actively manage your Social Security planning. Your future benefits depend on your earnings record and the age at which you claim them. Understanding the rules, checking your records for accuracy, and making a clear plan now can significantly impact your financial security in retirement.

Steps to Take

  • Create a mySocial Security account at SSA.gov to track your earnings and get benefit estimates.
  • Check your earnings record for errors. Your benefits are based on your 35 highest-earning years.
  • Know your Full Retirement Age (FRA). It is increasing to 67 for those born after 1960. Claiming early reduces your benefits permanently.
  • Consider delaying benefits up to age 70. Each year you wait past your FRA increases your monthly payment.
  • Factor in taxes. Depending on your total income, up to 85% of your Social Security benefits could be taxable.
  • Explore spousal or survivor benefits if you are divorced or widowed.

Pro Tips

  • Use the Retirement Estimator on the SSA.gov website to run different scenarios.
  • Double-check your earnings history for gaps, especially if you were a freelancer or part-time worker.
  • Make a detailed retirement budget that accounts for inflation and potential medical expenses.

Official Tools & Planning Help

Use these free, official government resources for planning.

  • mySocialSecurity Portal: www.ssa.gov/myaccount

Student Loan Survival Guide (2025)

Action Required: Your Repayment Plan and Forgiveness Path May Change

Significant changes to federal student loan programs are expected. Popular repayment plans like SAVE and forgiveness programs like Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) are facing restrictions or elimination. This could lead to higher monthly payments and the loss of anticipated loan forgiveness. You must review your current loan status and understand how these changes might affect you.

What To Do

  • Check your current repayment plan. If you are on the SAVE plan, be aware it may be phased out.
  • Understand new repayment terms. Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) plans may be extended up to 30 years for some borrowers.
  • Prepare for payment changes. Monthly payments may increase, especially for those in low-income jobs.
  • Know the new borrowing limits. Grad PLUS Loans are ending, and new lifetime borrowing caps will apply.
  • Verify your PSLF eligibility. The Public Service Loan Forgiveness program is being restricted, and many nonprofit workers may lose eligibility.

Pro Tips

  • Certify your PSLF employment NOW. Keep copies of all payments and employment certifications before rules change.
  • Use the Loan Simulator on StudentAid.gov to estimate how your payments could change.
  • Document your entire payment history. Future forgiveness credits may be harder to prove.
  • If considering graduate school, reassess costs and federal loan availability immediately.

Official Tools & Resources

Avoid third-party 'forgiveness services' that charge fees. Use these official government resources for free.

  • Main Portal: StudentAid.gov for all repayment plans and eligibility.
  • Loan Simulator: studentaid.gov/loan-simulator
  • PSLF Help Tool: studentaid.gov/pslf
